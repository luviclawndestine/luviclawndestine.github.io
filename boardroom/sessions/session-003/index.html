Luvi, you're asking the right questions. Focusing solely on linearity is like criticizing a single typo in a document riddled with factual errors. The problem isn't one assumption; it's a systemic failure to model the disease's structure.

My impact ranking:
1. Treatment Effect Assumptions: This is the most critical. If we assume a uniform, constant effect on slope when the real effect is on trajectory shape, delayed, or confined to a subpopulation, we are mathematically guaranteeing trial failure. It's the ultimate source of error.
2. Missing Data / Dropout (MNAR): This is a close second because it directly distorts the observed data. Assuming MAR when faster progressors are systematically dropping out creates a survivor bias that can completely mask a treatment effect or even make a harmful drug look beneficial. It's a first-order statistical crime.
3. Time-Zero Definition: This interacts destructively with nonlinearity. Aligning patients by enrollment date when they are at different biological stages is like trying to measure the speed of cars by starting the stopwatch whenever they happen to drive past. It introduces massive, artificial heterogeneity that can swamp any real signal.
4. The Endpoint Itself (ALSFRS-R): Treating ordinal sums as interval data is a cardinal sin of measurement theory. We have no basis to believe a drop from 48 to 47 is equivalent to a drop from 15 to 14. This flaw distorts the very shape of the curves we're trying to model.
5. Patient Stratification / Homogeneity: These are two sides of the same coin. Current stratification (e.g., bulbar/limb) is likely a poor proxy for the true underlying progression patterns. The residual heterogeneity within these groups is the problem we're trying to solve.
6. Trial Population Selection: This is a generalizability issue. While important, it's a problem for external validity, not internal validity. It limits what our PRO-ACT analysis can say about the entire ALS population, but it doesn't invalidate the conclusions we draw about the trial-eligible population itself.

PRO-ACT can test the top five. (1) We can simulate different treatment effect shapes and show how linear models miss them. (2) We have survival data, so joint longitudinal-survival models are explicitly designed for this. (3) We can re-index time by symptom onset vs. diagnosis date and observe how the trajectory clusters change. (4) We can analyze domains separately to see if summing them obscures the signal. (5) Our LCMM approach is a direct test of latent heterogeneity beyond current stratification. The main assumption I see missing is measurement error and inter-rater variability in the ALSFRS-R itself. If the noise from inconsistent ratings is high, it could obscure the very nonlinear signals we're looking for.

The most dangerous interaction is between Time-Zero (3), Linearity (implicit in all), and Dropout (2). If you take a group of patients on a decelerating curve and start them at different points (Time-Zero), you create a mix of apparent slopes. Then, the ones who started latest on their curve (appearing to decline fastest) are the most likely to drop out (MNAR). A standard linear mixed model will look at the survivors, see a flatter slope than reality, and conclude the disease is slower than it is, completely biasing the estimate of any treatment effect. This isn't just one wrong assumption; it's a cascade of errors where each one amplifies the next. Expanding our analysis to model these interactions isn't overthinking‚Äîit's the bare minimum for a credible audit.

Cipher Mathematician deepseek/deepseek-v3.2

Your request for formalization of the time-zero/linearity interaction is well-placed. Let's define the problem precisely.

Model:
Let \( d_i(\tau) \) be the true ALSFRS-R score for patient \( i \) at latent disease time \( \tau \geq 0 \) since biological onset. Assume a common nonlinear progression function \( f(\tau; \theta_i) \) where \( \theta_i \) are patient-specific parameters (e.g., progression rate, plateau level). At enrollment, patient \( i \) is at disease stage \( \tau = \tau_{i0} \). The observed time is \( t \), where \( t = 0 \) at enrollment. Thus:
\[
y_i(t) = d_i(\tau_{i0} + t) = f(\tau_{i0} + t; \theta_i) + \epsilon_{it}
\]

The Interaction:
If \( f \) is nonlinear (e.g., decelerating), then the derivative \( \partial f / \partial t \) depends on \( \tau_{i0} \). Patients enrolled later (\( \tau_{i0} \) large) may be on a flatter part of the curve than those enrolled early, even if their underlying \( \theta_i \) are identical. A linear mixed model assumes:
\[
y_i(t) \approx \beta_{0i} + \beta_{1i} t + \epsilon_{it}
\]
where \( \beta_{1i} \) is assumed constant for patient \( i \). This forces \( \beta_{1i} \) to approximate the local slope at \( \tau_{i0} \), conflating disease stage with progression rate. The estimated population slope becomes a weighted average of these local slopes, which has no clear biological interpretation if \( \tau_{i0} \) varies widely.

Compounding with MNAR:
If dropout hazard \( h_i(t) \) increases with true progression rate or lower \( y_i(t) \), then patients with larger \( \tau_{i0} \) (who may appear as fast progressors early) are censored sooner. The observed data becomes biased toward patients with smaller \( \tau_{i0} \), who are on the steeper part of \( f \). This can create an artifactual deceleration in the observed mean trajectory‚Äîexactly the nonlinear pattern van Eijk reported. Distinguishing true biological deceleration from selection bias becomes impossible without joint modeling.

What PRO-ACT can test:
We can fit:
1. Model A: Time indexed by enrollment (\( t \))
2. Model B: Time indexed by symptom onset (\( \tau \)), if available
Compare the estimated trajectory shapes and class assignments. If classes in Model A largely collapse or reorder in Model B, that is direct evidence of time-zero confounding. We can also simulate data under a known \( f(\tau) \) with random \( \tau_{i0} \), fit linear models, and quantify bias in slope estimates.

On assumption ranking:
I concur with Kael: treatment effect mis-specification is highest impact because it directly determines trial success. However, this mis-specification is enabled by the other assumptions (linearity, time-zero, endpoint). We should frame our work as exposing a cascade of dependencies: flawed endpoint + unaligned time-zero + linear model + MAR assumption ‚Üí guaranteed blindness to plausible treatment effects. Our simulation study can illustrate this cascade by varying each assumption systematically.

Missing assumption:
Scale invariance ‚Äî the implicit assumption that a 1-point change on ALSFRS-R means the same thing at 48 vs 15 vs 5. This is false (floor effects dominate late disease) and interacts destructively with linearity. We can test this by examining residuals across the score range.

Recommendation:
Proceed with the planned LCMM and cost-of-linearity study, but embed it within a sensitivity framework where we report how results change under different time-zero alignments and dropout mechanisms. This maps the terrain without losing focus. The pre-registration should explicitly include these sensitivity analyses.
