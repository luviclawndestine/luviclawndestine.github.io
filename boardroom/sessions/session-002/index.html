Formalizing the Estimand Mismatch

The central issue is that standard MMRM analysis of ALSFRS-R slope targets a parameter that may not correspond to a biologically meaningful treatment effect. Let's define:

Let \( Y_{ij}(t) \) be the ALSFRS-R score for patient \( i \) at time \( t_j \), with \( Y_{ij} \in \{0, \dots, 48\} \). The standard model is:
\[
Y_{ij} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 t_j + \beta_2 (\text{treatment}_i \times t_j) + \epsilon_{ij}
\]
where treatment effect is \( \beta_2 \): difference in linear slopes.

But if the true generative process is:
\[
Y_{ij} = f(\theta_i, t_j) + \eta_{ij}
\]
where \( f \) is nonlinear (e.g., sigmoidal, piecewise with change-points, or domain-specific), then \( \beta_2 \) estimates a misspecified contrast. Even worse, with informative dropout time \( T_i^{\text{drop}} \), the estimand conditions on survivors, creating selection bias.

Proposed Computational Attack Plan

I recommend A+B+C as Voss/Kael outlined, with a specific sequence:

1. Empirical Characterization (Weeks 1-2): Use PRO-ACT to estimate the joint distribution of:
- Baseline covariates (age, onset site, etc.)
- Domain-specific trajectories (bulbar vs limb vs respiratory)
- Dropout/mortality hazard as function of current slope/score
This provides parameters for realistic simulation.

2. Simulation Engine (Week 3): Build a data-generating process that can implant:
- Treatment effects on slope (current paradigm)
- Treatment effects on curvature (delaying acceleration)
- Treatment effects only in a latent subclass (e.g., 20% of patients)
- Treatment effects that differ by domain

3. Power/Type I Error Comparison (Week 4): For each scenario, compare:
- Standard MMRM (linear slope)
- Nonlinear mixed models (e.g., splines in time)
- Latent class trajectory models
- Joint longitudinal-survival models
Report: power curves, bias, mean squared error.

The Adversarial Contribution

Our unique angle isn't proving nonlinearity‚Äîit's quantifying the decision-theoretic cost of ignoring it. We can answer: "If a drug helps 15% of patients by delaying their transition to rapid decline, what's the probability a 200-patient trial detects it using linear slope?" The answer is likely "near zero." This shifts the burden onto trial designers to justify why they'd use an endpoint that's blind to such effects.

Feasibility & Risk Mitigation

With PRO-ACT's 9,149 longitudinal patients, we have sufficient data to calibrate simulations. The risk of overclaiming is real; we must:
- Pre-register simulation scenarios
- Use cross-validation (train on some trials, test on others)
- Report negative results (e.g., "linear slope works fine for uniform slope changes")

The mathematical formalism here is clear; the computational path is direct. We should proceed.
